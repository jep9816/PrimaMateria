Atomic Mass

Index

The average mass of all nuclides of an element determined by the proportions in which each nuclide of the element are present within the earth and its atmosphere. The Standard Atomic Weights apply to the elements as they exist naturally on Earth, and the uncertainties take into account the isotopic variation found in most laboratory samples. Further comments on the variablility are given in the footnotes.

The number in parentheses following the atomic weight value gives the uncercainty in the last digit.

An atomic weight entry in brakets indicates that the element has no stable isotopes; the value given is atomic mass in u (or the mass number, if the mass is not accuratly known) for the isotope of the longests half-life.

Thorium, Portactinium and Uranium have no stable isotopes, but the terrestrial isotopic composition is sufficiently uniform to permit a standard atomic weight to be specified.

Footnotes:

  • a: No stable isotope exists. The atomic mass in u (or the mass number, if the mass is not accuratly known) for the isotope of the longests half-life.
  • b: Commecially available Li materials have atomic weights that range between 6.939 and 6.996; if a more accurate value is reguired, it must be determined for the specific material.
  • g: Geological specimens are known in which the element has an isotopic composition outside the limits for the normal material. The difference between the atomic weight of the element in such specimens and that given, may exceed the stated uncertainty.
  • m: Modified isotopic compositions may be found in commercially availabble material because it has been subject to an undisclosed or inadvertent isotopic fractionation. Substantial deviations in atomic weight of that given can occur.
  • r: Range in istopic composition of normal terrestrial material prevents a more precise atomic weight being given; the tabulated value should be applicable to any normal material.
