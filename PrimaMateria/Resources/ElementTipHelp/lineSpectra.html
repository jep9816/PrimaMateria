In the 19th Century, some experiments showed that the structure of atoms must be far more complicated than had been previously believed. These experiments investigated the behavior of atoms excited in an electric discharge. When hydrogen and other gases such as helium are heated, the atoms absorb excess energy and the electrons undergo a transition from the lowest energy level, to a higher energy level. When the electrons fall from this higher energy level back down to the lower energy level, a photon of light is emitted. Each photon of light has a definite energy and a characteristic color. This emitted light is called the atomic spectrum (plural, spectra).

Passing the emitted light through a prism separates it into beams, each of a single wavelength and color. Prisms separate light by refracting it; when light of different wavelengths pass through a prism, it is bent at different angles. The fact that excited atoms emit light of only certain discrete wavelengths is evidence that their internal structure must be complex, otherwise there would be no reason why light of every wavelength is not emitted.

The information in the line spectra list is limited to the visible light spectrum. There are many more lines not shown in this list that are outside of human visibility.
